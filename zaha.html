Zaha Hadid

Zaha Hadid (1950–2016) was a pioneering Iraqi-British architect renowned for her futuristic and often radical designs. She reshaped architectural boundaries with her dynamic forms and daring structures, influencing contemporary architecture globally.

Hadid's work is characterized by fluidity, fragmentation, and the exploration of space in three dimensions. Her designs often feature sweeping curves and unusual angles, challenging conventional architectural norms while creating visually stunning and functional spaces.

Among her most famous projects are the London Aquatics Centre for the 2012 Olympics, the Guangzhou Opera House in China, and the MAXXI Museum in Rome. Each of these projects showcases her signature style of organic, flowing forms that interact harmoniously with their surroundings.

Beyond aesthetics, Hadid was committed to innovation in materials and engineering. Her collaboration with engineers and fabricators allowed her to realize complex geometries and experimental designs that would have been impossible in earlier eras.

Zaha Hadid's legacy continues to inspire architects, particularly women in the field, showing that architecture can be bold, experimental, and expressive. Her work demonstrates that daring design combined with technical expertise can reshape urban landscapes worldwide.

"There are 360 degrees, so why stick to one?" – Zaha Hadid
